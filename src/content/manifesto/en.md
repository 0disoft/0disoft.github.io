---
{ "title": "AI and Local Deliberation for Better Rules" }
---

Laws exist to manage conflict. But most laws we follow today didn't emerge from deep discussion. They came out under tight deadlines.

When people ask "why do systems so often miss the mark," they usually point to lawmakers' competence. But there's a more fundamental issue. When you apply a single standard across a society where millions live by different values, something's bound to break. Someone faces inconvenience. Someone else suffers real harm. It's hard to predict what ripple effects a minor provision might trigger decades later.

There's an expectation that AI will solve this. We need to recalibrate that expectation. AI isn't some authority that designs perfect rules. It's a tool that shows you where systems collide, where minorities get affected, using data. It can visualize what outcomes might follow when you introduce a policy. That's the scope of what AI can help with. The final call still belongs to people.

Applying that simulation to national legislation won't fundamentally change the structure. The more realistic possibility lies at the local level. Why must we impose identical rules nationwide? Instead of central authorities rolling out laws uniformly, what if each region could try rules fitted to its own context? Share what works with other regions. Revise what doesn't fit. When multiple regions run different experiments simultaneously, society as a whole becomes less vulnerable to a single flawed decision.

Time reveals which agreements work better. Regions that maintain biased or unreasonable rules may see population decline. Regions that build rational systems may attract people. When quality of life drops in a region, that itself becomes a signal to revisit the rules.

One line must be drawn. Majority opinion cannot be allowed to suppress minorities or restrict individual fundamental rights. Freedom of body, freedom of expression, freedom from discrimination. The state should maintain only this minimal framework and leave room for local autonomy beyond it. Freedom of movement matters especially. When a particular norm doesn't fit, there must be physical and institutional conditions allowing people to move to other communities or create new ones. Without that, local autonomy just becomes coercion at a smaller scale.

The model where central authority dictates every aspect of life is already showing its limits. What AI can actually do is set the stage. What moves to make remains a human decision.
